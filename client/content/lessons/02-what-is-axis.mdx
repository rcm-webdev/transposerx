# What is Axis?

The **axis** in an ophthalmic prescription describes the **orientation** of the cylindrical correction — the meridian along which no cylinder power is applied.

## The Axis Scale

Axis values run from **1 to 180 degrees**. The scale is measured counterclockwise from the patient's right:

- **180° (or 0°)** — horizontal meridian
- **090°** — vertical meridian
- **045°** — oblique, lower-right to upper-left
- **135°** — oblique, lower-left to upper-right

The axis is always written as a three-digit number: 090, not 90.

## What the Axis Tells You

The axis indicates where the flat meridian of the correction sits. The maximum cylinder power lies 90° away from the axis. For example, a prescription written as `+0.00 -1.00 x 180` applies −1.00 D of cylinder power along the **vertical** meridian (90° from the axis of 180).

## Axis in Transposition

When you transpose a prescription, the axis shifts by exactly **90 degrees**. This is because the meridian of maximum power and the meridian of zero power swap roles:

- Original: `-2.00 -1.00 x 045` → Transposed: `-3.00 +1.00 x 135`

The axis never exceeds 180. If adding 90 would give you 270, subtract 180 to stay within the valid range. If the result would be 0, use 180 instead — they are optically identical, but 0 is not used in clinical notation.

## Why Axis Errors Matter

An axis error of even 10–15° can significantly reduce visual acuity and cause the patient to experience distortion or tilt. An axis error of 90° produces a correction that actively worsens vision. Careful axis management during transposition is not optional — it is a patient safety concern.
