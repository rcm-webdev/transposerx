# What is Cylinder?

In an ophthalmic prescription, the **cylinder** value corrects for **astigmatism** — an irregular curvature of the cornea or lens that causes blurred or distorted vision at all distances.

## The Shape of Astigmatism

A normal eye is spherical, like a basketball. An eye with astigmatism is shaped more like a football — it has different curvatures along different meridians. This means light entering the eye focuses at two different points rather than one, producing blur.

The cylinder value in a prescription compensates for this unequal focusing power. Its sign — positive or negative — describes the convention used to write the correction.

## Negative vs. Positive Cylinder

There are two equivalent ways to write the same cylindrical correction:

- **Minus cylinder form** is preferred by most ophthalmologists. Example: `-2.00 -1.00 x 180`
- **Plus cylinder form** is preferred by many optometrists and optical labs. Example: `-3.00 +1.00 x 090`

Both prescriptions above describe the same optical correction. Converting between them is called **transposition**.

## Clinical Significance

When you receive a prescription written in one cylinder convention and the laboratory or instrument requires the other, you must transpose correctly. A transcription error in the cylinder value directly degrades the patient's corrected vision and can cause eye strain, headaches, and disorientation.

Understanding cylinder — what it corrects and why its sign can be expressed two ways — is the foundation for mastering transposition.
